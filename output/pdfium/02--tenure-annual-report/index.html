2023 ANNUAL REPORT

OUR MISSION: To work in partnership with Indigenous Peoples and local communities to strengthen their tenure and ability to preserve, protect and enjoy the benefits of their traditional lands, territories, and resources.

OUR VISION: Indigenous Peoples and local communities thrive in their ancestral territories with full recognition of their right to own, manage, and develop their traditional lands, territories, and resources.

OUR VALUES: We root our work in three core values: respect, reliability, and trust.

Tenure Facility was established in 2017. It is registered as a non-profit organisation in Sweden with a flexible team presence in countries across Asia, Africa, and Latin America.

Last year Tenure Facility’s partners succeeded in getting governments to formalise indigenous and community land and forest rights to over 2 million hectares and made progress on improving tenure security and governance in almost 22 million hectares, benefiting nearly 14,000 communities.

Behind these big numbers lie thousands of individual stories and successes, trials, and tribulations. You will find some in this main report, and hundreds more in this appendix.

We are well on our way to achieving our ambitious target of improving tenure security and governance in 60 million hectares by 2027 – an area one and a half times the size of California.

In 2023 we gave US$ 26 million to 32 projects in 18 countries. Thanks to our generous donors, we were able to double the number of projects we funded and the amount of onward grants we provided, compared to 2022. That contributed to much greater impact and laid the groundwork for much more to come.

We emphasise tropical rainforests critical to the global climate and biodiversity. Overwhelming scientific evidence shows that indigenous and community rights over and sound stewardship of these areas protect vital natural systems and help to stabilize the climate. Where Indigenous Peoples and other traditional communities manage forests and have strong rights over them, this reduces forest loss, safeguards natural habitats, and slows climate change. By protecting their territories, livelihoods and cultures, these forest guardians are protecting us and the planet.

We take gender equality seriously. We funded 7 major organisations led by women (3 of which are all-women organisations) and 3 projects focus entirely on women’s rights to land and forest. Almost all the projects we funded achieved notable results related to gender with regards to policies, tenure claims, leadership roles, organisational spaces, gender training and awareness, and livelihoods.

Our partners use the support we provide to 1) Push and partner with governments, to get them to officially recognise communal land and forest rights and support indigenous and community efforts to remain in harmony with nature; 2) Renovate and invigorate customary institutions, to protect communities and their natural and cultural riches from external threats, improve their wellbeing, and ensure women’s rights are respected, and youth can find their place; and 3) Strengthen their own organisations, to make them more effective, accountable, and inclusive.

Our policies and practices are tailored-made for our partners. We a) Fund partners to prepare proposals and consult with communities; b) Provide larger and longer grants than most – US$ 500,000 to US$ 1,500,000 a year with five-year grant agreements; c) Allow partners to respond to emerging challenges and opportunities; d) Customise our funding and reporting approaches based on partners’ capacities and contexts; and e) Respect rights and concerns about maps, photos, and stories.

We do not just give money. We provide strategic and technical advice, learning opportunities, and emotional support. Less year we co-sponsored six major regional and global learning exchanges, with 1,500 participants. We help partners get results at scale by facilitating new links to government authorities and other sources of funding, training, and solidarity.

We also help to systematise and share these experiences and work with the Path to Scale coalition, the Global Alliance of Territorial Communities (GATC), and other allies to make the case for greater and better funding for similar efforts.

Nonette’s message

MOVING FORWARD

We are living in turbulent times, and in an increasingly turbulent climate. We are in the throes of wildlife extinction, dwindling forests, run-away climate change, the loss of languages, and loss of the cultures that nurture this natural world.

But as a young person walking the forest path with my father, I learned that Indigenous Peoples, are securing their territories. Despite increasing pressures, they are protecting their forests, peat lands, and waterways – and our climate and the planet’s biodiversity. They can sustain these efforts and can do more. Save more forest. Live better and safer – with law, peaceful negotiation, and practical steps. But they need our help now.

They need more than quick fly-in fly-out cooperation to run the odd workshop. Our partners are wise and need wise collaboration.

That is why I am so excited to see how the Tenure Facility, since our founding seven years ago, is fulfilling its mandate – to dramatically scale and shore up Indigenous and community land and forest guardianship across the world.

2023 was a year of abundance and growth for us. This was thanks to our supportive donors, and the hard work of our partners. Our main partners include most of the major Indigenous Peoples, Afro-descendant, and community organisations that are protecting tropical forests, lands, and waterways across Africa, Asia, and Latin America.

Together we built on last year’s soil preparation, planting, and the favourable seasons. Our strategies became clear. Our systems stronger. More partners got their projects up and running. Many successfully expanded their impact, even with dark clouds on the horizon. We will enter next year with a summer of harvesting and richer results from this year’s growth.

With you, our allies, we helped secure and improve community tenure and governance in 21.7 million hectares of land and forests – three times our progress last year – benefiting almost 14,000 communities.

We are well on the way to meeting our audacious goal of 60 million hectares with secure land and forest tenure by 2027 – an area one and a half times the size of California!

We increased the number of projects we directly support. We have 32 large projects managed and implemented by Indigenous Peoples, Afro-descendant, and community organisations and a handful of allied NGOs. These projects span 18 countries. We exponentially increased Tenure Facility’s onward grant disbursal to projects and organisations. At the same time, we kept our central overhead slim.

The way forward is not straight. I was raised in the forests of the Philippines, where Indigenous Peoples and communities live. Early in my life I saw their territories and forests violently stolen by illegal loggers, miners, plantation companies, and drug dealers. Men, women, and young people died to defend the places they called home. My own friends were affected. As I write, many rural people, who are defending their forests and territories, still suffer death threats, rape, harassment, illegal evictions, and forced assimilation.

But that has not stopped them, and it won’t stop us. We move forward together, with them and with you. Together. For all of us.

BABASSU PALM FORESTS

(Attalea speciosa) cover 200,000 square kilometres across the Amazon Basin extending from Brazil to Bolivia, Peru, Guyana, and Suriname. They do not come immediately to mind when imagining the deep rainforest. But they are home to an impressive explosion of life, including macaws, parrots, monkeys, armadillos, anteaters, giant otters, river dolphins, and geckos. The palm absorbs carbon and funnels rainwater down its fibrous trunk, storing both underground, and providing moisture to the surrounding vegetation.

The oil from the babassu palm nut is rich in anti-inflammatory, anti-bacterial, and hydrating properties. The nuts provide a sustainable livelihood for hundreds of thousands of mostly Afro-descendant women in northern Brazil. After collecting the nuts, the women – known as nut breakers or quebradeiras – crack the nuts apart with small axes while sitting and singing together in a communal circle. Later, all the women share the profits of their labour. Like forest guardians all over the world, they protect the health of the babassu as if their lives depend on it. Because it does.

They are warrior women.

Four decades ago, cattle ranchers stopped the women from collecting the nuts. They burnt down and poisoned the palms to establish pasture. Some women paid a terrible price when they went to collect nuts – murder, rape, and the torching of their small wooden houses.

In response the women organised themselves into what is now one of the largest rural women’s movements in the world, the Inter-State Movement of Babassu Women Nut Breakers (MIQCB), with more than 300,000 women.

The Babassu women approached the Tenure Facility for support to expand Free Babassu Laws across more municipalities, enforce existing Laws, and get title to their forest lands; and in 2023 we gave them their first grant. We also started funding the National Coordination of Rural Black Quilombola Communities. Quilombolas are the descendants of people who escaped slavery by taking refuge in the forests. These two partners are working closely with national and local government agencies to secure legal title to 200 territories and protect millions more hectares and get proper government enforcement of the laws. With Tenure Facility support, they hope to leverage our funding to get additional finance from the Brazilian Bank for National Development (BNDES).

They persuaded 17 municipalities to pass Free Babassu Laws, protecting the babassu forests and giving the women quebradeiras full access to them. “Our struggle for rights is directly related to broader environmental goals,” said Maria Alaídes Alves de Souza, a coordinator of the MIQCB. “The whole world could learn from our relationship with nature. Protecting the forest means protecting food security, water, air, life for all of us. There are ways to live while protecting the forest and biodiversity. We, the traditional peoples, and communities, do that.”

It is a golden moment. The new Brazilian Ministry of Racial Equality is enthusiastic about issuing title to Quilombola lands. Brazil’s president, Luiz Inacio Lula da Silva, is eager to showcase his global leadership on forests and climate change in the run-up to the 2025 climate conference (COP30) that Brazil will host.

The scientific community has spoken. Where Indigenous Peoples and other traditional communities manage forests and have strong rights over them, forest loss is reduced and climate change mitigated.

One World Resources Institute (WRI) study found that deforestation rates were two to three times lower in titled indigenous territories than in other Amazon forest lands in Brazil, Bolivia, and Colombia. They estimated the carbon mitigation benefits of the titled territories would be US$ 25 billion – US$ 34 billion over 20 years.1

Then the Inter-Government Panel on Climate Change (IPCC)’s first report on land and climate change in 2019 recognised the importance of securing community land for climate change.2 At least three subsequent UN reviews have reaffirmed that.3

Another research team demonstrated that across the tropics, indigenous territories reduce forest destruction as much or more than in protected areas, despite receiving much less funding.4

Project Drawdown calculated that by doubling the area where Indigenous Peoples have secure legal rights to forests could reduce global carbon emissions by 8.69 Gigatons of CO2 equivalent by 2050. That equals about one fifth of the world’s annual CO2 emissions.

2023 witnessed a virtual explosion of similar reports, with more than 20 peer-reviewed studies pointing in the same direction. A global review of 320 econometric studies found that Indigenous Peoples and community forest management were two of the variables most consistently associated with less deforestation.5 Another study of over 300 sites in 15 countries in Africa, Asia, and Latin America showed that where rural communities have formal control and practical influence over forests, the forests store more carbon, have greater biodiversity, and provide better livelihood outcomes.6

Two Contradictory Trends And High Stakes For The Outcome

While there were many dark clouds on the horizon in 2023 and real cause for alarm, there were also positive signs. Which prevail, will depend on all of us. And the stakes could not be higher.

One the downside, Latin America’s Indigenous and Afro-descendant Peoples, communities, and forests faced a litany of threats from ranchers, gold miners, oil companies, soya bean farmers, loggers, drug traffickers, fires, and infrastructure projects. Even some communities with land titles suffered land invasions, fires from nearby farms, or mining or logging concessions imposed without consent. Progress in Africa was stymied by large new oil, mining, and oil palm deals, Jihadist violence and coups, and armed militias in the Congo. In Asia increasingly authoritarian national governments prioritised large-scale coal, minerals, and oil projects, oil palm and pulp plantations, and logging over social or environmental concerns, and cracked down on groups who dissented.

The United States and Europe were distracted by horrific wars in Ukraine and Gaza, political uncertainty, and high inflation. Parts of Europe experienced a backlash to domestic climate policies as consumers and farmers fretted about how these might affect their pocketbooks.

Meanwhile, the global climate crisis showed troublesome signs of spiralling out of control (record heatwaves, fires, floods).

On the upside, deforestation rates plummeted in Brazil and Colombia after respective presidents Luiz Inacio Lula da Silva and Gustavo Petro enacted positive forest and land policies. Suriname’s parliament debated the country’s first law enshrining Indigenous and Tribal Peoples’ land rights. The Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC) implemented favourable policies such as local community forest concessions and an Indigenous Peoples’ Rights Law. Kenya and Liberia slowly moved ahead on recognising communal land rights. Asia saw an uptick in government recognition of customary forests in Indonesia and Forest Rights Act implementation in a few states in India, as well as an on-going commitment to community forests in Nepal.

Within the mixed panorama on climate action, the relatively narrow objective of securing community land and forest tenure – a proven and cost-effective approach with outsized benefits – was a source of inspiration.

The global five-year US$ 1.7 billion Forest Tenure Pledge, signed by donors in late 2021 at the climate conference (COP26) in Glasgow, continued to resonate and highlight the need to recognise and reward Indigenous Peoples and communities for their role as nature’s guardians. Donors who signed the pledge and others boosted their funding.

Public interest and awareness grew as the world began to wake up to the opportunities for tapping into the courage, leadership, and knowledge of the likes of the women babassu nut breakers and the tens of millions of other forest guardians around the globe.

What makes the Tenure Facility unique?

We specialise in funding major Indigenous Peoples (IP), Afrodescendant (AFD) and traditional community organisations in the pan-tropics, with our operating systems specifically designed to respect their rights and meet their needs. We help IP, AFD, and traditional community organisations to connect to governments, and to other sources of funding, training, and solidarity. Helping our partners develop strong collaborations with others helps them get results at scale.

We pay special attention to gender equality and youth inclusion, and to cultural sensitivities. The community organisations with which partner are diverse, as are their leaderships; they involve the old and young, women, and minorities.

At the Tenure Facility, we also work at the global level with partners and allies to get key donors and intermediaries to scale up their support for IP, AFD, and other community organisations, in ways that meet their needs.

Our funding and support help indigenous and community organisations to both push and partner with governments.

Communities can also strengthen control over lands by participatory mapping, preparing community land use and conservation plans, documenting local knowledge and passing it from one generation to the next, monitoring forests for fires and illegal miners and loggers, resolving conflicts informally, and agreeing on boundaries with neighbours.

Pushing or pressing governments can consist of anything from calling on them to design or implement policies, regulations, and procedures (without violating U.S. lobbying restrictions), taking them to court, or simply helping communities to prepare claims and applications. In most places we work, communities must jump through and over many hoops and hurdles to get land tenure or forest certificates approved. For communal land and forest rights to reap their main social and environmental benefits, the customary authorities that manage the resources must be well-organised, accountable, and inclusive, and have clear rules and responsibilities. PARTICIPATORY MAPPING, PLANNING AND MONITORING

One of the most important ways communities can gain effective control over their traditional territories is to get together to take stock of what they have, how they have been using it, and decide what they want to do going forward. That leaves them much better positioned to make claims and negotiate with governments, companies, international funders, and NGOs, address unwanted incursions, and implement other collective activities.

This goes way beyond drawing lines on a piece of paper or preparing formal documents. It is a chance to work collectively, see their surroundings in a new light, learn from each other, reaffirm their identity, and build consensus. It is especially important to capture the unique knowledge and perspectives of elders, women, youth, and minority groups.

Sometimes participatory maps and plans are an initial step towards obtaining formal land rights, public funding, or permits to harvest forest resources. Other communities use them for their internal purposes or to negotiate with neighbours or companies.

This is a major area of many of our partners’ work and they do it well. Besides overseeing participatory processes, about one dozen have formal Geographical Information Systems or work with other organisations who do.

By far, the largest mapping undertaking in 2023 was that of the BRWA ancestral domain registration agency in Indonesia. PROTECTED AREAS

A surprising share of the projects involved community rights in and around protected areas. In Bolivia, Brazil, Guatemala, Panama, and Peru, and mostly recently the DRC, partners helped to create large biological corridors by ensuring that communities, which respect and protect the forests, have secure rights to areas next to parks. In Brazil and Peru, COIAB and indigenous human rights and environmental group FENAMAD supported reserves for uncontacted and recently contacted Indigenous Peoples. Indigenous local governments such as the South Rupuni District Council in Guyana and the two recently recognised Autonomous indigenous Governments in the Multiethnic indigenous Territory and Cavineño territories took on responsibilities for conservation in their territories and where protected areas overlap. Indigenous confederations CONAIE and CONFENAIE in Ecuador and COONAPIP in Panama worked to title indigenous lands in protected areas, while respecting their integrity.

Partners in the DRC and Kenya addressed evictions of indigenous villagers from parks and ensuing conflicts. And, as noted previously, Guatemala’s community forest concessions manage large areas forest sustainably in the multiple use zone of the Maya Biosphere Reserve in the Petén.

Building trust between communities and parks authorities can take time. But, when successful, rights-based conservation leads to better outcomes. Indigenous elders can impart their knowledge to community youth, who then use new geo-spatial and information technologies to alert parks authorities so they can jointly control illegal incursions. This kind if of collaboration is needed to keep the forest standing, revitalise indigenous culture and traditional knowledge about conservation, and build confidence along the way.

Three new projects in 2023 focused largely on ensuring indigenous and community rights and improving relations between communities and park authorities in large blocks of tropical rainforests, where indigenous and other forest communities overlap with protected areas. These included the CREF network and Tropenbos DRC projects in North Kivu in the DRC and the FENAMAD Project in the Madre de Dios River Basin in Peru.

Even though these projects only recently began, the CREF Network, which aims to conserve and restore forests, has already reached agreement with the director of the Maiko National Park in North Kivu to collaborate on community participation in park demarcation and development of its new 10-year management plan. This has opened a path for potential comanagement with communities of approximately one million hectares adjacent to the park.

BOX 13: WOMEN’S WAY

It is no coincidence that half the leaders elected in the newly recognised indigenous autonomous self-governments in Bolivia were women. Many mixed-gender partner organisations have focused on gender-parity policies, ensuring 50/50 women/men representation.

In all projects women are stepping up and out in front of village gatherings and discussions with government, elected as leaders in their organisations, participating in mapping activities, joining their territory’s environment defender network.

PARTNERSHIP WITH GOVERNMENT AND COMPANIES

Practically all our partners collaborate with some national or local government agencies. Typically, both sides achieve much more together than they could on their own.

The organisations we work with inform government policies and procedures, prepare paperwork so governments can process claims, train government officials, design apps and other software used by government agencies, facilitate government visits to communities, provide intelligence about illegal activities, fund events co-sponsored by governments and government travel to meetings. Ironically, when there is great political instability, constant rotation of agency staff, and chronic loss of public documents, our indigenous, Afro-descendant, and community partners often serve as the State’s institutional memory.

At the same time, our partners depend on government to issue titles and certificates, approve land use and management plans, remove illegal occupants, protect local leaders, regulate extractive activities, and collect data.

Sometimes partners need to take governments to court to get them to act. About half of our projects reported using national or international courts successfully last year. For example:

LESSONS LEARNED

The differences in context between regions (and sub-regions within them) are too great to use the same approach everywhere. Our approaches must vary depending on the level of political will and space for civil society, the institutional configuration of government decision-making and policy implementation, the types, and capacities of partners available to work with, and the presence and influence of various types of donors. BOX 17: LESSONS LEARNED ABOUT PARTNERSHIPS

Women’s collectives and womenled organisations represented a quarter of our partners in 2023. Their achievements further demonstrate their unique power to broker agreements, resolve entrenched conflicts, and design solutions that result in more equitable outcomes.

As we expanded the number and complexity of our partnerships in 2023, we learned a great deal, and some key lessons emerged:

  • Democratic decision-making within our partner organisations can be slow, but there is a lot to be said for democracy. Democratically elected indigenous or community leaders have greater credibility, legitimacy, political influence, and capacity to mobilise community resources. They are more responsive to their constituents.
  • More than half of our projects involve multiple partners. Multi-party projects require the partner organisations to create clear, formal governance arrangements from the outset. They tend to work best when the organisations have already worked together for some time and there is trust-based history for collaboration.
  • To achieve lasting change not just for men but for everyone in the communities, we need to redouble our support for women’s collectives (e.g., CNAMIB, MIQCB, UEFA) and organisations led by women (e.g., AMAN, KPA, CONAQ, APA, Podáali).

We discovered the wisdom of the African proverb: “If you want to go fast, go alone. If you want to go far, go together.”

The level of support from government agencies tends to fluctuate over time. When discussing potential projects with partners it is useful to consider different scenarios in that regard and how they might adjust. In general, in cases where central government agencies have become less supportive it has been necessary to consider approaches that communities can undertake on their own, options involving sub-national governments and alternative national government agencies and using international agencies and other stakeholders to communicate with and influence government officials.

Our partners have a growing demand for reliable accurate and understandable information and advice about carbon markets. We must find ways to respond to that demand, often by identifying experts and reference materials to which we can refer partners.

As threats to the Amazon continue to mount, alliances between those committed to its conservation and indigenous organisations are of increasing importance. Although there is a history of conflict and mistrust between indigenous and Afro-descendant groups and some conservationfocused organisations, when they join forces, major wins can be achieved. The fight against rollbacks for protection of Uncontacted Indigenous Peoples in Voluntary Isolation in Peru mentioned above is one example, as was the landmark referendum in Ecuador last August that banned oil drilling in Yasuní National Park.

We learnt that we must pay more attention to disability. In many communities where we work, mercury contamination of waterways used for daily fishing, drinking, and washing has unleashed a growing epidemic of disabilities.

Within the Tenure Facility team itself, we are also challenged to understand how so-called disabilities are perceived or valued within our partners’ cultures. One culture may view as impediments and mock, for example, cognitive changes linked to aging, but other see it as the wisdom of elders. Blindness and abilities to wander into other dimensions are often hallmarks of spiritual leaders and shamans, held in high regard, and cared for. These are dimensions we must explore with humility.

SECTION 5:

How?

Our Special Sauce

Tenure Facility is not just any financial mechanism. It specialises in providing funds, managing grants, and facilitating partnerships in ways that are specifically suited to the needs of indigenous, Afro-descendant, and community organisations and the contexts in which they work. These are ways that are “fit for purpose.”

We have not got all the mix of ingredients right in our sauce, but here are some parts of the recipe which worked well this year.

Our first few partners came to us through longstanding relationships they had with the Rights and Resources Initiative, an organisation that incubated and spun off the Tenure Facility. It had identified them as effective and well-respected organisations in their countries, and had built trust with them over years, often before any funding.

In 2021, we adopted a more formal system, to identify countries with good opportunities and the best strategies and potential partners for promoting indigenous, Afrodescendant, and community land and forest rights. This includes a combination of gathering information, analysing and having dialogues with potential partners. The feedback from partners was that this worked reasonably well in 2023.

We first do an initial “scan” of a country and, if things look promising, follow up with thorough country assessments, which give a good sense of who we might work with on what. In countries where we already work and know the relevant players well, we sometimes skip this step and open dialogues with potential partners.

Our board then approves our country assessments and initial project ideas to make sure they fit our strategies and principles. Having diverse board members that come from the constituencies we serve helps to make us more accountable. In 2023, we completed country assessments in Gabon and Republic of Congo (Brazzaville), and began others in Cambodia, Lao PDR, and Vietnam.

Prospective partners design the projects themselves, which are usually centred on their on-going work related to land and forest rights, territorial governance, gender equality, and youth inclusion. Once they have an agreed concept note and we have looked at their financial management, we sign a five-year partnership agreement and fund them to prepare a full proposal and consult with the communities involved to make sure the proposed project has their free, prior, and informed consent. We visit the partners physically, face to face in their offices, and in some of the forests and territories where participating communities plan to carry out activities – in the jargon of some funders – “project appraisal”. But we prefer to see this as the beginnings of “accompaniment”.

If a group does solid work and has strong support from their constituencies, they can still manage a project even if they are not so experienced at administering funds. In that case, they need to find a fiscal sponsor to administer their funds, but they still call the shots. In 2023 about one third of the projects had fiscal sponsors of various sorts. In some cases, this was temporary, until the group could manage its own funds.

Practically all the projects include activities to help partners strengthen their organisations. Most of these activities are proposed by the organisations themselves to meet their own objectives, although a small part of that may be to help them do a few things they need to do to manage a larger Tenure Facility supported project. Most partners are keen on those as well, because they are already looking how to scale up their efforts.

We listened to what our partners have had to say on all of this.

As a result, this year we continued to make the process and requirements of agreeing grants as clear and simple as possible, including simpler ways for partners to share with us what results they are achieving with Tenure Facility grants.

We have adapted our financial assurance or due diligence requirements to types of organisation and contexts. Our partners are not monolithic organisations. They work at multiple levels, have varying governance and organisational structures, and speak multiple languages.

Working in remote forest regions, far from capital cities, such as in eastern DRC, brings challenges – few or unstable banks, few or no roads, long distances, weak digital connections, few people giving receipts for goods and service. In 2023, we commissioned a study on best practices for cash management in settings like these to be finished next year. We will also learn from the experiences of community territorial funds as to how they successful operate in such conditions.

We take care to respect our partners’ sovereignty over the data, maps, stories, and photos they produce. To get the details of that right, in 2022 we commissioned a study on the issue and then last year held a workshop with academics and partners to discuss the results. That allowed us to have a clearer understanding with partners on this, and with our donors.

They include Indigenous leaders, development practitioners, human rights experts, and finance specialists. For full biography see: https://thetenurefacility.org/about-us/our-team/board-of-directors/

Myrna Cunningham, the Board Chair, is a Miskitu leader from Nicaragua. She is a medical doctor, former chair of the United National Permanent Forum on Indigenous Issues, founder ex-rector of the University of the Autonomous Regions of the Caribbean Coast of Nicaragua (URACCAN), and former Governor of Nicaragua’s northern Caribbean Region. She has chaired many international boards and received numerous international awards and honorary degrees.

Augusta Molnar from the United States served most of 2023 as Board Secretary and head of the Project Selection Committee. She previously serves as Director of Country and Regional Programmes at the Rights and Resources Initiative (RRI) and Senior Natural Resource Specialist at the World Bank and Chairwoman of the Board of the Mountain Institute.

Similarly, Donald Roberts was Board Treasurer for most of the year. Don is the President & CEO of Nawitka Capital Advisors and has over 30 years’ experience as a financial services executive, investment bankers, and equity professional.

Other Board Members included: Eleni Kyrou, a Greek economist who chairs the governance committee and is Senior Impact Programme Officer at the European Investment Bank (EIB); Abdon Nababan, former Secretary General of AMAN in Indonesia; Joan Carling, a Kankanaey sociologist from the Philippines, who serves as Executive Director of Indigenous Peoples Rights International (IPRI); Juan Martinez, a Mexican anthropologist working at the Inter-American Development Bank (ADB); Albert K. Tenure Facility’s Executive Director, a human rights lawyer from the Philippines, also serves as an ex officio member of the Board.

OUR TEAM

At the end of 2023 the Tenure Facility team was composed of 56 people from diverse backgrounds, half of whom work part-time. (So, an equivalent of 40 full-time people.)

We are organised in four units (programme, finance, operations, and development) plus the Executive Director’s team. Within the programme unit, there is a sub-unit focused on Monitoring, Evaluation, and Learning. Three regional teams, each with its own regional coordinators, draw on members from these four units.

In 2023 Tenure Facility continued its trend of decentralising its programme team to the regions where our projects are based. During the year we had team members in one dozen countries (up from eight in 2022): six in Latin America, four in Africa, and two in Asia. This allowed us to be close to partners in the key countries where we worked and intimately knowledgeable about their diverse cultures and political economies.

During the year, we created a new Development Unit, responsible for donor relations and communications, and brought on a new lead for Strategic Partnerships, several new financial controllers, and a new specialist in Monitoring, Evaluation, and Learning.

We also contracted Arthan human capital advisory services in India to help refine our global Human Resources policy to ensure that we have the diverse talent that we need, policies are clear and comply with relevant legislation, and all team members are treated fairly and well.

What Next?

As we looked back over the year, many things inspired us. The new generation of powerful women leaders outperforming their predecessors and taking what’s rightfully theirs. Seeing scientists and so-called experts finally catch up with what Indigenous Peoples have known forever – that they are the Guardians of the forests. The hunger among many philanthropies to find new ways to relate with people that they fund. The growing list of relevant governments willing to embark upon this journey. Local leaders who put their lives on the line every day, but never cease to smile.

It has been hard to imagine and internalize how quickly Tenure Facility has grown and matured, from an idea in a few peoples’ heads to an international organisation with extensive networks and a formidable footprint on the ground. But even more amazing, the best is yet to come.

Almost half of our projects are just taking off and the earlier projects are coming of age. This year we just began new projects in Cameroon, Peru, and both Congos, and we expect to launch several more. We may not be able to double again the area and number of communities where we see advances this year. But we are confident that our recent investments will yield high returns.

More formal land and forest rights. Organised and vocal communities. Mapping and monitoring. People and parks. Women and girls. New and surprising partnerships, with unusual suspects. And greater global desire to learn and assist.

The up-coming Biodiversity Summit (COP 16) in Colombia in October 2024 and the Climate Summit in Brazil (COP 30) are especially propitious. Indigenous Peoples, Afro-descendants, and communities will be high on the forest and climate agenda. With strong social movements mobilizing to make their voice heard and governments willing to listen. They offer moments to reflect on the progress since the US$ 1.7 billion pledge of support for this agenda and to lay out a framework for moving forward.

No. The challenges won’t go away. The dark clouds continue to haunt us, and breaking news distracts. We know that there will be setbacks.

We also know that while the task could not be more urgent, it won’t happen overnight. These are inherently lengthy processes. Forests don’t grow in a day. It takes time to talk things through, and bureaucracies are not known for speed. While we count and savour each short-term victory, this will take a little patience.

Nor do we naively believe there are simple solutions to these very complex problems. But we do believe that those closest to the problems that affect them are best able to develop the solutions that work. Since they have taken up the mantle, we are there to lend a hand.

And, so far, it seems to be working.

When we report back next year, we hope you will be with us again. It means a lot to us, and the world around us. And it might just surprise you.
