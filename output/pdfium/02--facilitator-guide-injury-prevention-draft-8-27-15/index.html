INTRODUCTION

Facilitator’s Guide for Injury Prevention in Public Health

Welcome to the Injury Prevention in Public Health Facilitator’s Guide. This document is designed to support facilitators in delivering structured and engaging sessions using previously recorded videos. It provides session objectives, discussion prompts, and key takeaways to ensure a comprehensive learning experience.

These structured learning sessions were created in collaboration with Diné College and consist of 10 prepared lectures that fit into 10 courses. Each lesson plan also includes a recorded lecture introducing the basics of Injury Prevention as a field of study. Further details of the development and evaluation of this collaboration effort can be found in the publication {INCLUDE CITATION WHEN READY}.

Learning Session Structure Overview

  • Target Audience: Public health undergraduate and graduate programs
  • Duration: 70 Minutes
  • Format: Facilitator leading the session withtwovideo presentationsandinteractive discussions.

Facilitator Preparation

  • Review all pre-recorded videos and supplementary materials.
  • Ensure you have the facilitator slides.
  • Familiarize yourself with key concepts and discussion prompts.
  • Ensure technical setup for video playback and audience participation.

Small Group Discussion Tips

Many student participants may not turn their cameras on in the zoom discussions, and we do not suggest that you request they be turned on. We recommend starting with a welcome and using names when inviting responses to help create a more personal and engaging atmosphere. Admit that you may pronounce names incorrectly, and that you would like to be corrected if an error is made. Inform them that you will start by asking everyone to respond to the first question and state the question again (to give some additional time for the first responder to have a response).

Begin with the provided starter question and go around the room, inviting each person to share. Feel free to gently prompt quiet participants or encourage responses in the chat if needed. Alternate the discussion questions between having everyone respond and having volunteers respond. Do not be afraid of silence... this is when thinking is happening. After a period, state the question again, thank the audience for the time of reflection, and invite responses either voluntarily or in the chat.

Introduction to Public Health

Ideal Course for Introduction to Public Health: Risk & Protective Factors

This lesson is designed for an introductory course that provides a broad overview of the field of public health, with a focus on promoting health and preventing disease within communities. Students would explore core public health concepts, including epidemiology, health policy, environmental health, social and behavioral sciences, and global health. Emphasis would be placed on health equity, Indigenous health perspectives, and the unique health challenges and strengths of Native communities.

This lecture fits into the introduction to public health course described above as it expands the discussion about risk and protective factors to include those related to injury prevention. This session also has a specific focus on environmental health risk factors in injury prevention.

b. Conduct the introduction activity: Zoom Poll OR Waterfall Multiple Choice

The activity is outlined in the slides, but here is the activity description.

THIS NEEDS TO BE SET UP AS A ZOOM POLL. As the details for setting up a zoom poll (or alternative remote meeting software) may change with time and updates, this will require some preparation ahead of the lecture. Set up the poll using this multiple-choice question (also on the slide).

Which of the following is an example of an environmental risk factor that can
increase the likelihood of unintentional injury?
A) Adequate street lighting in residential areas
B) Presence of broken or uneven sidewalks
C) Use of safety gates at the top of stairs
D) Installation of smoke alarms in all bedrooms

Give a few moments for everyone in the class to respond to the poll and encourage guessing (if the answer is not known) as the responses will be anonymous. Close the poll and present the results. Provide the correct answer of “B.” Comment on the type of responses given and that everyone should be more familiar with the phrase “environmental risk factor” after the lesson.

An alternative is to invite the students to select an answer and put it in the chat. Use the waterfall approach and have them write their choice but press enter at the same time when you say “go.” After the waterfall, provide the correct answer of “B.” Comment on the type of responses given and that everyone should be more familiar with the phrase “environmental risk factor” after the lesson.

Possible extension activity: If there is good engagement, an extension of the activity would be for the students to enter in their responses to the verbal question “What is a term to describe the other selections, A, C, and D?” Desired response: Protective Factor.

b. Conduct the introduction activity: Zoom Waterfall

The activity is outlined in the slides, but here is the activity description.

The "waterfall" activity on Zoom is a simple, engaging way to gather input from all participants at once. You, the facilitator, will pose a question and ask everyone to type their response into the chat box—but not to press "Enter" yet. After giving everyone a moment to type, the facilitator prompts participants to hit "Enter" at the same time, creating a "waterfall" effect of responses flowing into the chat all at once. This technique encourages participation, reduces response bias, and allows quieter voices to be heard equally.

An example script to use for the activity:

"Let’s do a quick waterfall activityto hear from everyone at once. Please take a moment to type your answer to this question in the chat—but don’t press Enter yet:

‘What are some of the policy issues you have discussed so far in your course?’

Once you’ve typed your response, just wait. I’ll count down and then we’ll all hit Enter together to ‘release the waterfall’ of responses.

Ready? 3… 2… 1 … Go!"

Comment if any injury prevention topics show up in the chat. Transition to the lecture by suggesting that the next time you ask this question, perhaps there will be more injury related responses.
