The Rutgers-Eagleton/SSRS Garden State Panel

The Rutgers-Eagleton/SSRS Garden State Panel is the largest probability-based opinion sharing platform in New Jersey. It is comprised of a representative group of panelists randomly selected throughout New Jersey who have agreed to share their opinions through surveys.

The panel was established in 2023 as a joint research venture between The Eagleton Center for Public Interest Polling at the Eagleton Institute of Politics at Rutgers-New Brunswick (ECPIP) and SSRS. Both organizations are members of the AAPOR (American Association for Public Opinion Research) Transparency Initiative and committed to full compliance of AAPOR standards and transparency.

The Rutgers-Eagleton/SSRS Garden State Panel Methodology

The Rutgers-Eagleton/SSRS Garden State Panel is a probability-based panel of New Jersey adults aged 18 or older. Members are recruited randomly based on statewide representative ABS (Address Based Sample) design. ABS sample is drawn from the Delivery Sequence File (DSF) maintained by the U.S. Postal Service. Population coverage of the DSF is in the 98%-99% range.

During the recruitment process, demographic information on panelists is collected. This data is stored securely and used to determine eligibility for specific studies (if needed). We also rely on these data to improve the survey experience for panelists by avoiding the need to re-ask some demographic questions with each survey.

How are Garden State Panel surveys conducted?

The Rutgers-Eagleton/SSRS Garden State Panel is a multi-mode panel (web and phone). Most panelists take self-administered web surveys; however, the option to take surveys conducted by a live telephone interviewer is available to those who prefer to complete surveys by phone and/or who do not use the Internet. Panelists also have the option of taking surveys in their preferred language (English or Spanish). We translate and conduct surveys in Spanish as required by specific projects.

Samples are drawn among panel members to best meet the study criteria. Selected panelists are sent an email invitation to participate in the survey, including unique survey link. The link allows the respondent to complete the self-administered online survey. Our contact protocol also includes a text message survey reminder for panelists that have consented to receiving them. All telephone interviews are conducted live using a CATI (Computer Assisted Telephone Interviewing) system. Interviewers ask to speak with the person at that number by name who is a member of the Garden State Panel.

SSRS web surveys comply with industry best practices. They are optimized for smartphone/mobile device administration and are adapted to modern operating systems and browsers.

How is the Garden State Panel an improvement over opt-in panels?

The Rutgers-Eagleton/SSRS Garden State Panel is a fully probability-based panel because it recruits randomly selected panelists from a probability-based sample source that reflects the New Jersey adult population. Results obtained from this panel can statistically represent the target population with a known margin of error.

Unlike typical opt-in panels, no one can “volunteer” to be part of the Garden State Panel. They must be selected randomly and invited to participate. By employing rigorous sampling methods, we not only reduce the risk of selection bias but also minimize susceptibility to “bots” or fraudulent panelists. This additional layer of security enhances the reliability of our survey results, providing accurate insights into New Jersey’s diverse populations.

Sample Design

About the Rutgers-Eagleton/SSRS Garden State Panel Rutgers-Eagleton/SSRS Garden State Panel members were recruited randomly via an addressbased sample (ABS) survey of New Jersey residents aged 18+. ABS respondents are randomly sampled by Marketing Systems Group (MSG) through the U.S. Postal Service’s Computerized Delivery Sequence File (CDS), a regularly updated listing of all known addresses in the U.S. For the SSRS/Rutgers-Eagleton Garden State Panel, known business addresses are excluded from the sample frame. Respondents of the original survey were asked if they would like to be invited to further surveys, and those who indicated yes were added to the Garden State Panel.

Most panelists take self-administered web surveys; however, the option to take surveys conducted by a live telephone interviewer is available to those who do not use the internet as well as those who use the internet but are reluctant to take surveys online.

About the SSRS Opinion Panel
SSRS Opinion Panel members are recruited randomly based on nationally representative ABS design, including Hawaii and Alaska. ABS respondents are randomly sampled by Marketing Systems Group (MSG) through the U.S. Postal Service’s Computerized Delivery Sequence File (CDS), a regularly updated listing of all known addresses in the U.S. For the SSRS Opinion Panel, known business addresses are excluded from the sample frame. Additional panelists are recruited via random digit dial (RDD) telephone sample of cell phone numbers connected to a prepaid cell phone. This sample is selected by MSG from the cell phone RDD frame using a flag that identifies prepaid numbers. Prepaid cell numbers are associated with cell phones that are “pay as you go” and do not require a contract.

About L2
L2, Inc. is a national voter data company that maintains a comprehensive database of registered voters across the United States. The database integrates individual-level information from state voter files with appended demographic, household, and vote history data.

Survey Sampling
Sample drawn for this study from the SSRS/Rutgers-Eagleton Garden State or SSRS Opinion Panel Panelists included New Jersey residents ages 18 or older. Sample was drawn using a probability proportional to size (PPS) methodology to ensure adequate representation of each demographic group while minimizing the variability of the final weights. The sample was additionally stratified by preferred survey language, mode, and race to meet the sample size targets for each group.

The oversample was drawn in part from the L2 national voter database. The oversample frame was built from national voter files maintained by L2 and designed to allow for oversamples of Black, Hispanic, and Asian respondents. Respondents’ race and ethnicity were identified through L2’s modeled race and ethnicity variables, and an oversample of Black, Hispanic, and Asian respondents was created. The sample was drawn from records that have a landline or cell phone number appended.

KEY TERMS, DEFINITIONS, AND GROUPINGS

Race and Ethnicity
For purposes of analysis, respondents’ race or ethnicity were categorized as either white, Black, Hispanic or Latino, Asian, or “other.” The “other” category includes those who identify as multiracial. For example, if a respondent indicates they are white and Black, they would be placed in the “other” category. Those identified as white, Black, or Asian also identify as non- Hispanic or Latino. Analysis in this report focuses on white, Black, Hispanic or Latino, and Asian respondents.
Region
ECPIP typically divides the state of New Jersey into five regions: Urban, suburban, exurban, Philadelphia area/South Jersey, and shore, based upon the state’s counties. Urban includes Essex and Hudson Counties. Suburban includes Bergen, Mercer, Middlesex, Passaic, and Union Counties. Exurban includes Hunterdon, Morris, Somerset, Sussex, and Warren Counties. South Jersey near Philadelphia includes Burlington, Camden, Cape May, Cumberland, Gloucester, and Salem Counties. And shore includes Atlantic, Monmouth, and Ocean Counties.
Parent Status
Respondents were asked whether they are or someone else is currently pregnant with their child or if they have a biological child. Those who indicated any of those experiences applied to them were included in a subgroup of parents and future parents that were asked about their experiences and/or plans during pregnancy and childbirth. Collectively, this group is simply referred to simply as “parents,” regardless of whether or not their child was already born.

KEY TAKEAWAYS

Discrimination and Everyday Interactions

  • Most New Jerseyans do not frequently feel personally discriminated against because of their race or ethnicity, but experiences diverge sharply by race. A plurality of Black residents say they "sometimes" feel discriminated against both by other individuals and groups and by the state's laws and institutions (46% in both cases), while a majority of white residents say they "never" feel this way (42% by individuals, 51% by institutions).
  • Nearly 3 in 10 residents (29%) believe Black people in New Jersey face "a great deal" or "a lot" of discrimination, but views vary dramatically. Black residents are more than twice as likely as white residents to hold this view (56% vs. 22%), and Democrats are far more likely than Republicans to agree (40% vs. 15%).
  • When it comes to improving racial equality in the United States, residents place the most responsibility on the news media: 81% say the media has at least "some responsibility," more than any other institution or group tested, followed by the federal government (76%), the New Jersey state government (75%), and local government (70%).

Personal Health and Healthcare Access

  • New Jerseyans are generally able to get healthcare when needed, though mental health care lags significantly behind other services. At least 80% were able to get care when needed for prescriptions (88%), medical issues (83%), and dental issues (80%), but only 64% say the same for mental or emotional health care. Access disparities exist by income and education.
  • Residents tend to view their own community as a healthier place to live than New Jersey as a whole: 36% call their community "very healthy," compared with 18% who say the same about the state.
  • A majority of residents, 71%, believe some New Jerseyans have less of an opportunity to lead a healthy life than others, while 25% believe all residents have an equal opportunity. Democrats, Black residents, and Hispanic or Latino residents are more likely to perceive unequal opportunity.
  • Residents place primary responsibility for ensuring people can lead healthy lives on individuals themselves (87% say individuals have at least "some" responsibility), more than on any level of government. This tension between individual and structural responsibility runs throughout the survey's findings.
  • More than a third of residents (36%) believe one's own race or ethnicity has a "major influence" on a person's ability to lead a healthy life, with Black (46%) and Hispanic or Latino (42%) residents substantially more likely to say so than white residents, among whom a plurality say "minor influence" (43%).

Healthcare Inequities

  • Two in 5 New Jersey residents believe individuals are treated inadequately or unfairly in healthcare settings "all" or "most" of the time based on their health insurance status (41%), while 1 in 5 feel the same when it comes to race or ethnicity (20%), gender identity (16%), or sexual orientation (12%).
  • A quarter of residents believe racial discrimination interferes with access to quality healthcare in New Jersey at least "a good amount," with Black residents substantially more likely to hold this view (45%) than white (22%), Hispanic or Latino (34%), or Asian (18%) residents.

Housing

  • Homeownership rates divide sharply along racial and income lines. 80% of white residents own a home, compared with 46% of Black residents and 46% of Hispanic or Latino residents. Feelings of neighborhood safety follow a similar pattern: 66% of white residents feel "very safe" in their neighborhood, compared with 40% of Black residents.
  • Housing barriers are widespread: one-third of New Jerseyans have faced barriers obtaining affordable housing, 3 in 10 have faced barriers obtaining desirable housing, and 16% have faced barriers obtaining safe housing. These disparities are pronounced across party affiliation, gender, race and ethnicity, age, and income.
  • Among those who have faced housing barriers, cost overwhelmingly dominates as the primary obstacle: 70% cite cost as the most significant barrier, while only 3% cite discrimination. This framing of housing as primarily a cost problem, rather than a discrimination problem, is notable given the racial disparities in homeownership documented in the same survey.
  • More than 8 in 10 residents believe housing costs in their community make it difficult for some residents to pay for other necessities, and 78% say some New Jerseyans have a harder time obtaining affordable housing than others.
  • Residents place primary responsibility for ensuring access to safe and affordable housing on state, local, and federal governments, and one-third say increasing homeownership opportunities for all should be the state's top housing priority.

Maternal and Child Health

• Most parents report having had regular access to medical care during pregnancy and rate the quality of that care as "excellent," though perceptions of care quality differ by gender, race and ethnicity, and income. White residents are more likely than Black (48%) and Hispanic or Latino (49%) residents to rate their prenatal care as "excellent" (68%).

  • Six in 10 residents, regardless of parental status, believe some pregnant people have better access to quality prenatal care than others in New Jersey. Democrats, women, and higher-income residents are more likely to perceive this inequity.
  • Three in 10 residents say race or ethnicity has a "major impact" on infant mortality, but views are sharply divided. Black residents are about twice as likely as residents of other races and ethnicities to say race or ethnicity has a major impact (56% vs. 27% of white residents). A plurality of Republicans say race has no impact (36%), while a majority of Democrats say it has a major impact (39%).
  • When asked about causes of the higher Black infant mortality rate in New Jersey, residents are far more likely to attribute it to individual factors than to systemic ones. 71% say poor health of the person carrying the child is a cause and 64% cite personal choices of parents or caregivers, while only 40% attribute it to bias of healthcare professionals or the healthcare system. Black residents are much more likely to identify systemic bias as a cause (69% vs. 32% of white residents).

Policy Proposals

  • Thirty-eight percent of New Jerseyans say New Jersey's Family Leave Insurance Program does not go far enough, while 40% say it does just the right amount and 12% say it goes too far. Women and Democrats are more likely to say the program falls short.
  • New Jerseyans broadly and strongly support policies aimed at improving maternal and infant health outcomes. Strong majorities "strongly support" increasing availability of contraception or birth control methods (65%), an annual state review of maternal and infant mortality causes and rates (60%), and expanding access to health coverage for those uninsured during and after pregnancy (57%), among other approaches tested.
  • Nearly half of residents say Medicaid should be expanded to cover more people (47%), a view held more strongly by Democrats, Black residents, Hispanic or Latino residents, residents under 50, and urban residents. By contrast, 18% say Medicaid should be cut.
  • Residents are more supportive of Medicaid work requirements than of SNAP work requirements. 54% support the recently passed federal Medicaid work requirement law, while 49% oppose the law's new SNAP work requirements. Adding information about how many people stand to lose benefits moves opinion only modestly.

DEI Rollbacks and Connection to Health Equity

Conclusion

The findings of the 2025 RWJF Health Inequities Survey reveal that a majority of New Jersey residents recognize health outcomes are not equally distributed across the state's population, yet views on the causes of those disparities and the appropriate policy responses to them vary considerably by race and ethnicity, party affiliation, gender, income, and education.

On the question of what shapes a person's ability to lead a healthy life, large majorities point to access to affordable healthcare, having a steady, good-paying job, and personal health practices as having a "major influence." Fewer residents say one's own race or ethnicity has a "major influence," though this view is held more commonly by Black and Hispanic or Latino residents than by white residents. Similarly, while a majority of residents say some New Jerseyans have a harder time leading a healthy life than others, residents place the greatest share of responsibility for ensuring healthy lives on individuals themselves, more than on any level of government.

These patterns carry through to more specific findings. When asked about the causes of the higher death rate of Black babies born in New Jersey, residents are more likely to point to individual-level factors, such as poor health of the person carrying the child and personal choices of parents or caregivers, than to systemic ones, such as bias of healthcare professionals or the healthcare system. Black residents are substantially more likely than residents of other races and ethnicities to identify systemic bias as a cause, a gap that reflects a broader pattern throughout the survey in which Black residents are more likely than others to perceive structural drivers of health disparities.

Partisanship is a consistent dividing line across the survey's topics. Democrats are more likely than Republicans to say various levels of government bear significant responsibility for health and housing outcomes, to support Medicaid expansion, to say DEI programs help level the playing field, and to believe the legacies of slavery continue to affect the position of Black New Jerseyans today. On several questions, partisan differences exceed 30 to 40 percentage points. At the same time, broad bipartisan majorities support a number of specific policy interventions, including expanding access to health coverage during and after pregnancy, mandating workplace leave policies, and conducting annual reviews of maternal and infant mortality rates in the state, suggesting areas of potential common ground even amid wider disagreement on structural questions.

Racial and ethnic differences in perceptions are similarly pronounced. Black residents report higher rates of personal discrimination, greater belief in the structural causes of health disparities, deeper concern about the impact of DEI rollbacks on health outcomes, and stronger support for reparations than residents of other races and ethnicities. On the question of reparations specifically, Black residents are the only group where a majority strongly supports the policy (58%), while 60% of white residents are strongly opposed. These differences in perception and experience are themselves a substantive finding, as they speak to the degree to which the state's residents hold divergent understandings of how race shapes health, housing, and opportunity in New Jersey.

Taken together, these findings offer a detailed accounting of public opinion on health equity in

New Jersey at a moment when many of the policies and programs most relevant to that goal are under active national debate. The results document where residents agree, where they diverge, and how demographic characteristics shape those views, providing a foundation for researchers, advocates, policymakers, and communicators working to understand and address health inequities across the state.
