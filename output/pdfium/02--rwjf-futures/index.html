DEMOCRATIZING THE IMAGINATION OF THE FUTURE

We cannot create what we cannot imagine.

Lucille Clifton, poet

FOREWORD

The future is not fixed.

Anticipating, creating, and influencing possible tomorrows starts whenever someone asks “What could happen?” and, more importantly, “What do we want to make happen?”

Futuring is not about trying to predict what will happen. It’s about observing our present through a lens of what’s possible. It’s also about dreaming alternative trajectories. It’s about recognizing that the stories about tomorrow that we are telling today can become the blueprints for possible tomorrows.

It’s about expanding who gets to imagine the future.

When we engage more people from more places and more walks of life to imagine futures together, we open ourselves up to possibilities that would never emerge from current dominant perspectives becoming visions that could transform society.

The future is not a destination we’re traveling toward—we create it together, every day, with every choice, every story of tomorrow we tell, every possibility we dream.

We think the world is ready for more equitable futures.

A NOTE OF WELCOME

Seven years ago, I was having coffee with a new colleague at Robert Wood Johnson Foundation (RWJF). I wanted to tap her network of movement leaders as I looked to fill a program officer position on the Ideas for an Equitable Future (aka “Futures”) team I direct. This team, I explained, is tasked with anticipating, adapting to, and influencing the future toward a north star of health equity. We fund projects—including the work of professional “futurists”—to help spot signals of change and emerging trends and understand how those larger shifts in society might impact RWJF’s work toward a future where health is no longer a privilege but a right.

I told her I was looking for someone curious, creative and… “you know, someone who spends their time thinking about the future.” She nodded and then said something that made me stop and think: Lori, to be honest, I don’t think many in my network feel they have the time to think about the future in that way. They’re focused on current crises. For them, putting out fires and making immediate progress is more urgent than anticipating and envisioning the future.

I let her words sink in. They resonated, not just as sound insight for someone seeking to build a robust team, but also as something a team focused on the future needs to consider in the context of our grantmaking—that while everyone has the ability to imagine and contemplate the future, few leaders may have the access to the time, space, and support to engage in futuring. I shared this hunch with my team.

As the pandemic erupted and the future became more difficult—and important—to anticipate, my team and I explored how to enable more people to engage in futuring to advance health equity. We heard, anecdotally, that many organizations working toward social justice did not have the resources to work with a futurist or the time to engage in futuring. Even if they did, finding futurist facilitators with expertise or personal experience related to their organizational goals was challenging.

We began to provide support to leaders and organizations that would allow them to bring their futuring practices to community-serving organizations, thereby helping those organizations anticipate and imagine the equitable futures they were advancing toward.

We don’t need to reimagine the world. We need to imagine the world. With reimagination we inherit and try to build in the confines of our knowledge and existence. In BIPOC communities… often that’s trying to fix something that was never intended for us to flourish in the first place.

David Bickham

As we expanded the number and range of futuring activities we funded, I saw social workers in Oregon, members of the Future of Nursing committee in Washington D.C., youth in Jackson, Mississippi, and a group of rural leaders across the country open their eyes wide as they began to understand the implications of emerging trends—shifts in society that have the potential to help or hinder wellbeing for all. I saw energy rise as co-created visions revealed inspiring futures worth fighting for and surfaced steps these organizations could take now to advance into those futures. I began to see how important it was for all organizations and communities to have the time, space, and tools to anticipate, adapt to, and influence the future.

One day on a call with Kamal Sinclair—who, at the time, was directing the Guild of Future Architects, one of the organizations we funded—I heard her talk about their work as “democratizing the imagination of the future.” This phrase really struck me. I realized this was the strategy that had been revealing itself for the past two years.

As the team’s work in what we were now calling “democratizing the imagination of the future opportunity space” progressed, we supported more futuring leaders whose personal experience with structural racism informed their approaches. Several of these projects focused on social justice, art, community organizing, and imagining liberatory futures.

As a learning team, we supported futuring practitioners who experimented with many different approaches. Grantees developed, adapted, and applied new and established methods, tools, and strategies. They engaged communities with games, books, and dance parties in their workshops. Not only did these futuring activities enhance the effectiveness of community-led organizations, they also helped staff across the Foundation experiment with sensemaking and trend-sensing.

This report synthesizes what the Futures team at RWJF has learned about what is needed to promote futuring that advances equity for the fields of public health, racial justice, philanthropy, and foresight, and why and how that matters.

And it introduces you to 13 brilliant futuring practitioners and several other grantees who are shaping our collective visions of more equitable futures. We think their words, advice, and work will inspire you to look to their perspectives, tools, and methods for insight as you consider your strategies for impact.

What was an “aha” moment to me in 2019—that an important way to elevate inclusive equitable futures is to support the conditions for community members to take time to imagine what is possible—should not be groundbreaking to you in a post-2020 world. Presently, many in our country recognize that systemic racism underpins inequity. Futuring is also having a moment in the philanthropic community and beyond. There is no better time to dream up new systems resilient to the inequities than today—as the status quo is being so disrupted by political, cultural, and technological shifts.

What I hope will be YOUR “aha moment” is that there is something you can and must do today to create these conditions for change. Once we understand we are living out the imaginations of those who came before us, we owe it to future generations to have the people who best understand the challenges of structural racism and the potential of health equity leading the way.

I look forward to hearing what you do to influence the future!

Lori Melichar

Senior Director, Ideas for an Equitable Future team, RWJF

ABOUT THIS REPORT

The stories we tell ourselves about how the future will unfold affect what we do—or don’t do—today.

They shape strategy. They drive action. Whose narratives are we listening to? Whose assumptions about what is possible limit or expand how we are dreaming up our tomorrows, our neighbor’s tomorrows, our field’s tomorrows?

In this report, we make the case that to get to healthier tomorrows, we need to resource more people, communities, and organizations today with the time, space, and expertise to imagine futures beyond the constraints of oppressive dominant narratives and perceived limits of what is possible.

After seven years of grantmaking that explored what it means to democratize the imagination of the future, we stepped back and reflected on the body of work. We did this to inform our future funding strategies, and to surface and share learning that might be helpful to our grantees. As we began to understand the various ways a set of RWJF grantees have been futuring, we soon realized we were gathering a rich parcel of insights about how these practices benefit individuals, communities, and the fields of futuring, philanthropy, and racial justice and health equity.

Our Hope

By sharing what we and our grantees have learned about the need to democratize the imagination of the future, we hope to:

  • Inspire our philanthropic peers to incorporate futures thinking practices into their own work; support organizations in this pursuit by providing them with time, money, and space to do the same; and fund practitioners with a diverse set of experiences across the field who are advancing tools and methods of speculative visioning
  • Inspire leaders in racial justice and health equity to explore how such approaches might help them to interrogate their strategies, engage their communities, cultivate hope, prepare for the future, and develop bold interventions and compelling narratives
  • Inspire people, movements, and organizations across the social sector to take up the charge to spend time anticipating the impact of emerging trends, conceive of the futures they want to inhabit, build resilience through creativity, and discover new ways to work with others to bring those futures to life

Read on to learn about and from different approaches to futuring, and join us as we question our terms, explore the unique value of these practices, and discover links to tools and methods to go deeper.

How We Wrote It

Robert Wood Johnson Foundation’s Ideas for an Equitable Future team (aka the “Futures” team) commissioned Jessica Clark—founder and executive director of strategy and foresight firm Dot Connector Studio, and the Foundation’s previous Futurist in Residence—to take a deep dive into insights from leaders of a set of grant-funded projects focused on futuring, innovation, and emergent approaches to social change.

Clark was a foresight strategist and author who spent the past decade working hand-in-hand with many of the country’s largest foundations to understand how society, media, and technology are evolving—and how to design programs and productions to meet the moment. She spent four years embedded in the Futures team as RWJF’s Futurist in Residence, a consultant, and a participant-observer.

Dot Connector Studio’s research associate Jenna Rines joined Clark to conduct one-on-one interviews with leaders affiliated with current and past RWJF grants. Dot Connector Studio’s team also conducted field and desk research related to foresight, strategic visioning, social change, racial equity, and philanthropic practice. Then, they invited interviewees and other grantees to group discussions to elicit feedback on the research. Their analysis was informed by numerous conversations with RWJF’s Lori Melichar, senior director, and Jody Struve, senior communications officer, as well as conversations with the larger Futures team.

We also invited philanthropic peers inside and outside of the Foundation to weigh in on how democratizing the imagination of the future might be relevant to their work. Many thanks to Alicia Bell of Borealis Philanthropy, David Hsu of Omidyar Network, and Maya Ingram of Democracy Funders Network for their insights. Many thanks to the Dot Connector Studio team who helped this project come to fruition, including Katie Donnelly, Collin Riggins, and Nina Spensley. Thank you to Ghost Note Agency who designed this report. Their director of strategy, Sarah Duarte, was also a key thought partner.

As a result of this highly collaborative and iterative sensemaking process, the insights in this report represent dialogue with the interviewees, and many months of synthesis and revision. In the end, the core research team involved Clark, Melichar, Struve, and Rines. This report reflects the points of view and learning of the core research team, and through direct quotes, the points of view and learning of the interviewed grantees.

MEET THE INTERVIEWEES
Throughout the report, we share and celebrate the experiences, observations, and wisdom of

17 current and past RWJF grantees

whose work offers perspective on ways to democratize the imagination of the future. The interviewees hail from a variety of disciplines and practices—they are researchers, artists, organizers, social workers, inventors, and more—and many of them also work in novel and interdisciplinary ways.

Clark interviewed a core set of grantees who identified futuring, speculative fiction, or foresight as central to their practice or funded projects.

David Bickham

Award-winning author and poet, founder and chief futurist at Bickham MetaFutures, LLC

Bickham co-led an RWJF grant (2023–2025) to Jackson Medical Mall Foundation to make futurism and creativity an essential component in realizing health equity by codifying and disseminating Bickham’s Prophetic Innovation Consortium framework. This framework reimagines corporate, Western, and some Afrofuturistic paradigms to establish a “new world” of strategic foresight informed by the African American and African Diaspora and universally progressive causes. By strengthening the futuring muscles of leaders, systems, and institutions, this project explores how futurism studies can support institutional, systemic, and policy-related change, and how knowledge can be made accessible and move beyond the few current practitioners.

Lonny J Avi Brooks, PhD

Professor in strategic communications and foresight at California State University, East Bay, co-founder AfroRithm Futures Group, co-producer Afrofuturist Podcast

With support from an RWJF grant (2022– 2025) to Oakland’s Museum of Children’s Art (MOCHA), Brooks co-led, with Nina Woodruff, a project at MOCHA’s Community Futures School to prepare students aged 14–17 to be resourceful and contributing inhabitants of the future. Through community-building, futures literacy, and arts-centered practices, teens “investigate[d], reimagine[d], and identif[ied] solutions that build a more anti-racist, healing-centered, and inclusive Oakland in 2045.” Across the city, students showcased their visions of the future— futures where people like them thrive.

Creston M. Burse, MBA, PhD

Strategic foresight practitioner, technology agent, founder and chief strategy officer at Bemae Group

Burse played a key role in an RWJF grant (2020–2022) to the Jackson Medical Mall Foundation to promote the success of Jackson, Mississippi, public school students as innovators, futurists, and leaders through the Young Futurists Project. As the project’s Foresight Instructor, Burse helped integrate a health and futurism lens into its STEAM education program. The Young Futurists Projects sought to nurture young innovators in a health innovation culture and build capacity for young innovators and leaders to address current, emerging, and undiscovered issues related to health equity.

adrienne maree brown

Emergent Strategist, New York Timesbestselling author, social justice facilitator

With her network of collaborators, brown “nurtures Emergent Strategy, Pleasure Activism, Radical Imagination, and Loving Correction as ideas and practices for transformation.” She co-led an RWJF grant (2021-2023) to build the capacity of the Emergent Strategy Ideation Institute (ESII). Responding to COVID-19, economic instability, anti-Black racism, white supremacy, and patriarchal violence, ESII worked to seed and proliferate experiments with Emergent Strategy to address the lack of cohesiveness and other challenges facing the social justice movement. She was joined in her interview by her collaborator, Caitlin Breedlove.

Lafayette Cruise, MCP

Urban planner, futurist, adjunct professor at Rhode Island School of Design

Cruise leverages the “radical imagination and world-building capabilities of speculative fiction and the multidisciplinary, strategic implementation tools of urban planning in order to imagine, plan, and build a more just, liberating and sustainable future.” He was a founding member of the Regenerative Leadership Team of the Guild of Future Architects (GoFA), which received an RWJF grant (2021–2022) to scale up and equip a diverse collaborative of futurists embedded in communities with the resources and relationships to imagine, prototype, and realize just and inclusive futures.

Marina Gorbis, MPP

Executive director of the Institute for the Future (IFTF), author of The Nature of the Future: Dispatches from the Socialstructed World

IFTF has received a number of RWJF grants and RWJF has been a member of IFTF’s Vantage Program in past years. As a Vantage member (2022–2023), RWJF participated in IFTF’s Forecast Summit to examine evolving values about place and sustainable, equitable growth. An earlier membership (2020–2021) enabled RWJF to work with IFTF to identify emerging trends, and RWJF supported the development of the IFTF Future Factors platform to track signals of the future and connect futurists in a network. IFTF also received an RWJF grant (2021–2023) to map types of equitable enterprises and explore how they promote opportunity, wealth, security, and health for diverse workers and their families.

Karen Lord, MPhil, PhD, MS

Award-winning Barbadian author, editor of speculative and sociological fiction

Lord’s recent novel, The Blue, Beautiful World, was longlisted for the 2024 Women’s Prize for Fiction. She is co-editor of We Will Rise Again: Speculative Stories and Essays on Protest, Resistance, and Hope (2025). Lord’s story, “The Plague Doctors,” published in RWJF’s book of fiction, Take Us to a Better Place: Stories (2000), was selected for The Best American Science Fiction and Fantasy 2021. Lord served as RWJF’s Speculative Fiction Writer in Residence from 2023–2024. In this capacity, she explored the potential of speculative fiction as a cognitive tool to help RWJF staff imagine and chart paths to better, more equitable futures using dialogue and original fiction informed and inspired by sitting in on meetings, and workshops.

Trista Harris, MPP

Philanthropic futurist, professional speaker, founder and president at FutureGood

Harris served as RWJF’s inaugural Futurist in Residence from 2019–2021. With support from an RWJF grant (2021–2022) to FutureGood, Harris’ Field Trips to the Future program engaged a cohort of 30 rural and Native Nation leaders to strengthen the influence of future-oriented thinking in their work. Over the course of a year, the cohort discovered skills as future-focused leaders, explored resources and tools, and built relationships with a diverse set of professional futurists and each other as they advanced equitable rural development and community conditions for wellbeing.

Julia Mossbridge, MA, PhD

Futures shaper, author, cognitive neuroscientist, founder of The Institute for Love and Time (TILT), member of the Alfred Lee Loomis Innovation Council at the Stimson Center

With support from RWJF (2022–2023) to TILT, Mossbridge led a project to develop Time Machine, a “time-travel narrative” cross-platform web application that improves users’ future orientation—or the ability to habitually plan for a positive future. Designed by and for under-resourced people, it has been shown to improve overall wellbeing (as well as other measures related to wellbeing), especially for those who have survived trauma. A second RWJF grant (2024–2025) helped enhance the usability of and educate target audiences about the Time Machine app.

Laura Nissen, MSW, PhD

Disruptive foresight practitioner, former dean and professor emeritus at Portland University, School of Social Work, research fellow at Institute for the Future (IFTF)

Nissen led work on several RWJF grants (2022–2024) to bring social work practitioners into the world of foresight through creation of the Social Work Health Futures Lab and the 2024 Social Work Health Conference from the Future, which explored how foresight and futures thinking might influence the way that social work considers its work related to health and the social determinants of health. Before the Lab, the social work profession had only had cursory engagement with futures thinking. The goal of the conference was to increase the readiness and agility of social workers to meet the challenges of a rapidly changing future and disseminate learning and work from the Lab.

C. Brandon Ogbunu, PhD

Computational biologist, writer, assistant professor at Yale University Department of Ecology and Evolutionary Biology, professor at Sante Fe Institute

Ogbunu leads an RWJF grant (2023–2026) to explore cultural objects as model systems for the study of social inequalities. The goal of this project is to use the products of human culture—relics, institutions, and movements— to explore new research methods, data, and tools that can provide for a more modern understanding of social inequalities and create formal model systems to understand their dynamics. The project outlines three types of cultural relics, and proposes a means through which they can be used as models for examining and understanding the dynamics of social inequalities: 1) speculative fiction as an object for understanding inequities of the present and future; 2) sports as an institution to examine the dynamics of social inequalities; and 3) hip-hop culture as a source of public health data and messaging.

Aisha Shillingford, MSW, MBA

Artist, worldbuilder, experiential futurist, artistic director of Intelligent Mischief

Shillingford co-led, with Terry Marshall, an RWJF-funded project (2024–2025) to implement the Black Imagination Lab which uses the lens of reparations for Black people to imagine and build equitable futures. This research, design, and development program brought together a cohort of multi-sector leaders (arts, media, organizing, philanthropy, policy, governance, etc.) within and representing poor and working-class communities of color, and their allies, to build their imaginal capacity. Together they collectively imagined, designed, and developed worlds, stories, and experiences that reflected their visions of transformative futures that address the most intractable issues impacting the health and wellbeing of Black communities. They created reproducible tools and workbooks and story ideas that can animate and make this future world understandable. A “Museum of Repair” experience launched in 2025 presents a collection of artifacts, experiences, stories, and content to the general public.

Kamal Sinclair, MBA

Artist, producer, senior director of digital innovation at The Music Center in Los Angeles

Sinclair supports artists, institutions, and communities working at the convergence of art, media, culture, and technology. She previously served as executive director of Guild of Future Architects (GoFA) which received an RWJF general operating grant (2021-2022). Under her leadership, in the context of recovery and progress sparked by COVID-19, the national centering of racial justice, and unprecedented threats to democracy, GoFA helped people navigate disruptions at institutional, community, and national levels through practices of co-creation and complex inclusive design.

Why We Need More People Imagining More Futures

We are living in complex times that regularly challenge our assumptions and disrupt political and social systems.

That’s why it is important for all of us to develop “futures literacy”—a set of skills that, like reading and writing, anyone can hone. UNESCO calls futures literacy an essential competency for the 21st century, observing that “being futures-literate empowers the imagination. It enhances our ability to prepare, recover and invent in the face of change.”

Futuring doesn’t need to be an all-in, all-the-time activity. It can be something people or organizations dip in and out of—perhaps with help from people such as the interviewees featured in this report. Not every person in every organization needs to be futuring full time, and there are ways to build speculative visioning practices and tools into everyday routines.

Spending time in the future may sound abstract—and it can be. Spending time in the future can also be fun. Creating opportunities for joy, healing, and immersion, futuring can unlock new solutions and reveal emerging obstacles in ways that traditional strategic planning or organizing often doesn’t.

We offer five of the many reasons we have learned that we should all be spending more time with more people anticipating and imagining more futures:

The Future Is Ours to Make

To influence the future, we must develop a healthy relationship with it. We must not turn away from possible futures that worry or frighten us but turn toward the futures we want. Fighting oppression and inequity in the present is vital; spending time in the future we’re fighting for fuels the work. The alternative is to continue to inhabit futures that others assert for us that serve their own interests.

Why We Need More People Imagining More Futures

“Being here in Silicon Valley in particular, it is obvious that there are some people who basically think they are creating everybody else’s future and feel very empowered to create that future. Everybody else ends up living in these people’s futures… and it happens to them rather than happens in terms of something that they build.” —Marina Gorbis

The Future Shines a Light on Our Present

When we spend time imagining and anticipating futures where everyone thrives, we equip ourselves to analyze the present in critical and inspiring ways. Looking back from even a short visit to a liberatory future can help us see our emerging futures more clearly and put present inequities in stark relief. This can reveal the ways in which dominant narratives about what is possible often control or limit imagination.

“For so many of us, we can’t imagine what it means to not have those struggles. We know the fight; we don’t know what victory is.” —Lafeyette Cruise

The Future Can Help Connect Us

Unleashing imagination and working together to dream healthy futures can be a path toward talking across differences. Speculative visioning creates distance—a safer place for questioning assumptions and hearing others. When we are together in an imagined future, the stakes are not as high— and we may be more willing to play. And it’s energizing to be in a place of possibility: Leaving the present behind for a moment can ease pressure and friction.

“I think it’s actually fear of the future that’s driving a lot of the toxicity right now. If people actually felt they had a healthier relationship with the future and felt like they could at least talk to each other about it in a different way… if we could create space for people to dream together… it could bring the temperature down in a significant way… and allow us to imagine something quite different.” —Uma Viswanathan

The Future Needs Our Love

If we want to get to a sustainable future, we have to be in a meaningful relationship with it. Anticipating and collectively envisioning healthy futures takes time, care, and cooperation. We, individually and collectively, have to fall in love with the futures we want to emerge. When we do, we’ll want to take care of those futures, work hard to nurture our path toward them—removing barriers along the way so they become healthier places to inhabit, giving them what they need to thrive. We’ll want to take loving action today to keep that future alive and on track. Yes, it’s a long-distance relationship, and it has its ups and downs, but life just wouldn’t be the same without it.

“There are many futures that you could imagine. I don’t think you want to support people in imagining the future or helping others imagine the future in a way that promotes hate, divisiveness, inequity, or ill health.” —Julia Mossbridge

The Future Can Offer Hope and Healing

Engaging with the future in a critical and imaginative way can be refreshing and regenerative. It can help turn conversations from registers of despair to registers of hope. Placing ourselves in desired futures can also provide health benefits. It can connect us with our childhood selves—the time when we could imagine freely without judgment. Spending time in a liberatory future can be a part of a healing journey for traumas caused by structural bias.

“What is the culture in which people actually feel safe and they feel like their kids are safe? That’s a question I’m asking people all the time. It’s like: ‘Think of whoever’s your worst enemy. Now imagine that your grandchildren can play together. How do we get there so that they don’t feel scared of each other?’” —adrienne maree brown

Why We Need More People Imagining More Futures

We hope that our work will bring into being a future where we are living according to values of love, regeneration, and interdependence.

These values live in all of us but often our structures invite us to act more on values of domination, extraction, and violence. In this future we have repaired our relationships with ourselves, our communities, our land, and our ancestors. We live in abundant communities of care and mutuality. We have learned to slow down. To reconnect with the land around us. To deeply know and love our plant and animal kin, our land formations. We have become stewards, not owners. Feeling Our Way into Futures

What does it mean to feel our way into futures, why is it important, and how are our interviewees making it possible?

Often, strategic foresight is presented as an empirical exercise. However, feelings matter in this work. Efforts to rationally discuss possible trends and signals too often gloss over or ignore the inequities of the systems we live in and how those affect people.

In contrast, we can feel the futures we are exploring more intensely through stories and experiences that paint vivid pictures. Futuring processes that are co-creative and engage participants in exercising their own imaginations and drawing from the wells of their memories and perceptions are particularly powerful.

Novels, films, and games about the future that are embedded densely with characters, experiences, and dramatic twists can be compelling in ways that generate hope or foreshadow pivotal social changes. Some of the projects that interviewees we spoke with created build on common reference points from pop culture. Such approaches work only not because of the richness of description or depiction but the emotional resonance of the characters and the worlds being built— especially if they reflect our own identities and experiences.

Tangible or immersive experiences can help us to see what it’s like to inhabit and interact with a possible tomorrow. Some interviewees invite people to make artifacts that bring possible futures to life. We can travel to the shores of a possible future, pick up a metaphorical pebble, and bring it back with us to motivate our actions today. When we feel the futures we imagine, we remember them more viscerally, which can make us more likely to bring them into being.

Futuring can run the risk of being triggering—but also has the potential to be healing or cathartic. Many of these projects are unabashedly political. They illuminate the consequences of class, race, and power disparities. They reveal the long-term

Co-creation and play

Methods that bring people in to co-create narratives, worlds, characters, and artifacts use storytelling or art to explore possible futures:

Why might it be difficult for people to engage in futuring? While many studies suggest that humans are intrinsically future-facing, those seeking to invite conversations about the future sometimes encounter resistance.

Interviewees found that barriers can include:

  • Alienating jargon
  • Assumptions that futures thinking is about “predicting” the future
  • Concerns that futures thinking is too “woo woo” or mystical
  • Worries that spending time talking about the future ignores pressing present-day priorities
  • Feeling unqualified to imagine futures
  • Feeling uncomfortable or unqualified to be creative
  • Suspicion that talking about the future masks avoidance of or a desire to look past the inequities of the present
  • Depression or fear about the future, or a sense that there is “no future”

“When people see the worst of what’s happening right now, they shut down. How do we psychologically overcome the fear of the unknown and the pain of change?” asked Sinclair.

A key theme that threaded through our discussions with interviewees was: We know futuring makes a difference, but how do you tell that story?

Respondents reported that people who participate in activities to imagine better futures expressed a strong desire for more. They described the hunger people had for additional

engagement with futuring, especially in extremely difficult contexts or moments of crisis. They took pride in their visions.

Some said they experienced a mental shift that they could not un-see or un-know once they had the appropriate space and opportunity to engage their imagination and consider transformative change.

There are many questions about how best to evaluate both the short-term and long-term outcomes of this type of work. But the anecdotal nature of the evidence gathered from these varied projects, and what we heard from respondents, lifts up both a desire for more rigorous and standardized methods and the need to value a more human-centered mode of defining impact. Efforts to evaluate foresight and speculative visioning in various ways are already happening in both the futures studies and evaluation fields—for example, see the Summer 2024 special issue of New Directions for Evaluation, on bridging the two fields.

Bickham raised the urgency of this question for the work that he’s doing in Jackson, Mississippi. He noted that evaluation alone is not sufficient, however. Although futuring work is often collective, many of the impacts happen at an individual level. Lord shared that participants can leave her workshops with “a profound adjustment to their worldview or philosophy,” awe, and, what is jokingly called in speculative fiction, a “sens-a-wunda.” She shared: “That brush with the numinous can be an unexpected spark for new creative pathways to a solution, or new ways of seeing and being.”

Mossbridge’s work demonstrated the healing power of people forming a relationship with their past and future selves: “What that taught me is that simply realizing that you exist through time is really the first step to wellbeing,” she said. Beware of unintended harms: An important aspect of ethical and critical futuring practice is to consider and work to avoid possible unintended downsides of the work—especially in an increasingly polarized society. Making space for imagining a more equitable and diverse array of possible futures can come with risks. Possible concerns raised by some interviewees included ways in which:

  • Cultural or revolutionary language and concepts can become diluted and lose their original edge
  • Extractive corporate or political systems can co-opt speculative futuring ideas and turn what began as cautionary tales into harmful products or policies
  • Inviting people to co-create stories about the future that shine a critical light on the present can be seen as a threat to systems or ideologies, and draw hostile responses
  • Inviting visioning without resources to make progress toward those visions can cause mistrust and frustration

What We Learned, and What Comes Next

Democratizing the imagination of the future has never been more necessary.

As the systems that produce health collapse around us, many are seeking visions of a new media system, a new public health system, a new health science knowledge system. These new visions must be shaped by the brilliance of many—not just a powerful few.

Over the past seven years, we have learned so much from the futuring practitioners RWJF has supported—each trying to sense, imagine, and design more equitable tomorrows in dialogue with the communities they serve. These insights have bolstered our belief that when more people have the time and resources to imagine, we unlock the collective wisdom needed to create a healthier world.

By sharing what we and our grantees have learned in this report, our goal has been to show leaders, practitioners, organizers, and funders how the practice of futuring can help them anticipate the impact of emerging trends, build resilience through co-creation and creativity, conceive of the futures they want to inhabit, and discover new ways to work with others to bring those futures to life. We wanted to inspire more people across the social sector to spend time in the future.

We hope we have achieved this, and that you have arrived here ready to jump deeper into futuring. We hope you are asking: How do I turn this inspiration into action?

As artists, advocates, activists, and community visionaries, how do we take that next or first step on our journeys to imagining—and then creating—better futures?

As health equity and racial justice leaders and funders, how do we resource our communities in ways that provide them with the time, space, and expertise to imagine futures beyond their wildest dreams? Here are some possible answers:

GLOSSARY

Engaging in the futuring space exposes us to many approaches, movements, tools, and perspectives. Below are brief descriptions of some of the terms and phrases that appear in this report. We hope this list serves as a starting point to pique your curiosity.

Afrofuturism: A cultural and aesthetic movement blending imagination, technology, critical theory, and Black perspectives to envision liberated futures. Embracing science fiction and centering African diaspora culture and experiences, it can be engaged as a framework to rewrite history, challenge oppression, and contemplate possibilities beyond present conditions. [Sources: Womack, Brooks & Anderson, Brandon]

Anticipation: The sensing or expectation of future events, sometimes paired with preparation. [Sources: Copenhagen Institute for Futures Studies, Bourgeois]

Artifact: A tangible or conceptual representation (such as an object, text, or piece of art) of an imagined future, created to make that potential world more concrete, experiential, and accessible for reflection and discussion. [Sources: Institute for the Future, Candy & Potter, Candy & Dunagan]

Crip/Disabled Futurisms: A movement that
envisions futures centering disabled people, knowledge, and ways of living. It works to advance disability justice and counter ableism in the present and future. [Sources: Piepzna-Samarasinha, Neilson, Grist, Griffith, Society of Disabled Oracles]

Critical Futures: Approaches to futures practices that critique power, question the status quo, and challenge dominant frameworks and systems to work toward just and preferred futures. Critical Futures stand in contrast to predictive methods and approaches that focus on shaping a singular vision of “the future.” [Sources: Gidley, Slaughter, Inayatullah, Gidley]

Decolonizing Futures: Approaches to
futures practices that seek to dismantle the coloniality of foresight, interrogate visions of the future dominated by eurocentric and western perspectives, challenge assumptions and power that currently shape society, and go beyond inclusion to center a vast array of non-western perspectives, methods, and knowledge. [Sources: Krishnan, Bisht, Gesturing Towards Decolonial Futures]

Democratizing Futures: Expanding access
to fair and active participation in imagining and shaping futures, as well as going beyond inclusion to consider how power dynamics and systemic inequities impact participation. [Sources: Sitra, Barendregt et al]

Design Futures: Where design meets futures thinking, often using creative, speculative, and experiential methods to prototype and shape scenarios and possible solutions for desired futures. [Sources: Candy & Dunagan, Angheloiu et al., Kelliher et al.]

Futures: Emphasizing the plurality of possible futures, rejecting the singular “future” driven by the status quo in favor of diverse visions and alternative futures. [Source: Gidley]

Futuring: “Applied future exploration and action,” ranging from anticipation of perceived threats and opportunities, to envisioning how trends might shape next developments, to discovery of surprises or openings, to shaping of possible futures through “simulated experiences … via prototypes, media, artefacts, or immersion, to test-drive new realities.” [Source: Smith & Ashby]

Futuremaking: The act of exploring and negotiating a range of possible futures, and engaging in work to bring preferred futures into being. [Source: SSIR]

Futures Literacy: The ability to engage one’s imagination in various contexts to navigate and shape the future, understand why it is important to do so, and recognize underlying assumptions that might be involved. [Sources: Miller, Copenhagen Institute for Futures Studies]

Futures Studies: The field of study dedicated to exploring and understanding the range of potential futures (possible, probable, preferable) and the perspectives, values, and assumptions that drive them respectively. [Sources: Inayatullah, Copenhagen Institute for Futures Studies]

Futures Thinking: An approach to
strategically explore the range of possible futures using diverse tools, inform decisionmaking, and build capacity to foster preferable futures and transformative change in the present. [Sources: Inayatullah, Canina et al., IFTF]

Imagination: The ability to conceive of new possibilities, crucial for shaping futures and navigating uncertainty.

Indigenous Futurisms: A movement that
engages Indigenous traditional and ancestral knowledge, technologies, and culture to remix and expand past, present, and futures centering Indigenous people, narratives, and ways of being. Through a variety of different modalities, such as speculative fiction and art, it involves the interrogation of and recovery from impacts of colonization and oppressive systems to revitalize Indigenous culture and envision decolonial futures. [Sources: Dillon, Furtado et al., Kwaymullina, Killick]

Liberatory Futures: Refers to futures where interlocking systems of white supremacy, colonialism, and other oppressions are dismantled, and where the freedom and thriving of people most impacted by these systems are centered and affirmed. Liberatory futures are enacted through enduring imagination and action to resist, abolish, and transform harms that these systems cause. [Sources: Nassiri-Ansari et al., Wilson, Gumbs, O’Brien & Simon, Shillingford, Takla, Priyadharshini]

Participatory Futures: Approaches to
futures practices that expand participation in imagining and changing possible futures to the public, rather than limiting to policymakers and professionals. Intentionally including alternative points of view in envisioning and shaping future possibilities. [Sources: Nesta, Krishnan, Barendregt et al.]

Prototyping: Experimenting with, creating, and refining tangible ideas, products, and tools for a variety of future scenarios. [Sources: SOIF/California 100, Miller]

Radical Imagination: The ability to conceive of new possibilities outside of the constraints and structures of the present, and applying this to challenge oppressive systems and envision just, transformed futures. [Sources: Hill-Jarrett, Haiven & Khasnabish, Everyday Activism Network]

Queer Futurism: A framework for imagining futures that center LGBTQ2SIA+ culture, lives, and experiences, expanding possibilities for change beyond current conceptions of identity, and challenging dominant narratives through a queer lens. [Sources: de Paula, Larsen, Grist, Muñoz, Brooks]

Sankofa: A framework that derives from a word in Twi, which means to “go back and gather.” It entails rooting futures thinking in history and ancestry to create more informed visions of tomorrow. [Source: Preston]

Scenario: A story of how the future might occur based on key drivers, trends, and assumptions. [Sources: Copenhagen Institute for Futures Studies, Bourgeois, Amer et al.]

Scenario Planning: Developing and
presenting multiple stories about potential futures to inform strategic decisions and provoke critical consideration of alternative perspectives and possibilities. [Source: Amer et al.]

Signal: Emerging information, such as headlines, art, scientific innovations, or social behaviors, that is perceived and interpreted to consider its potential impact on future developments. Signals may vary in strength and are easier to interpret as more is known about them. [Sources: van Veen & Ortt, Gorbis, IFTF]

Speculation: Considering and imagining aspects of otherworldly or possible futures using a range of knowledges. [Sources: Hovorka & Mueller]

Speculative Fiction: An expansive and
diverse genre of fiction that explores alternative and nonrealist ways of being, provokes thought on possible futures, may include postcolonial perspectives and critiques of western values, and encapsulates a wide range of narrative fiction, like dystopias and utopias. [Sources: Gill, Oziewicz]

Speculative Visioning: Creating imaginative
futures to challenge assumptions and provoke new possibilities for action in the present. Includes an array of practices across several creative disciplines and encouraging participants to explore a range of futures in which we can all thrive. Modalities may include science fiction, design fiction to create futures artifacts, art, game and experiential design, and more. [Sources: This is a term that is not in wide use across the field, but that we are introducing here to call out the key role that imagination plays in inspiring people to take action toward better futures.]

Strategic Foresight: A discipline that applies a structured approach to anticipating possible future developments and planning for them. [Sources: Copenhagen Institute for Futures Studies, SOIF/California 100]

Trend: A general pattern of change over time; megatrends are large-scale, long-term shifts. [Sources: SOIF/California 100, Copenhagen Institute for Futures Studies; Cranfield University]

Worldbuilding: Crafting fictional worlds with detailed histories, cultures, and social structures. [Sources: Zaidi, Stackelberg & McDowell, Shillingford]
